\subsection{D-COMP: Dynamic Complexity Metric}\label{sec:8.10}

To quantify how complexity evolves as glyphs interact across dimensions, the ALQC uses a Dynamic Complexity (D-COMP) metric. While not explicitly named in the PDF, it is implicit in the quaternary resolution and M.A.S. calculations. We formalise it here to integrate the top-third content:

Let $(i,j)$ be a pair of dimensions with Q-state bias assignments Q$_i, q_j \in \{0,1,2,3\}$. For a glyph with Q-vector $G_{i,j}=[Q_0,Q_1,Q_2,Q_3]$, define the local complexity as:

\[
C_{\mathrm{local}}(i, j) = |Q_{q_i} - Q_{q_j}| + |Q_2| + |Q_3|
\]

Here $|Q_{q_i} - Q_{q_j}|$ measures the differential tension between the preferred channels of the two dimensions, $|Q_2|$ quantifies the shadow residue present in the glyph, and $|Q_3|$ captures recursive capacity. The global D-COMP is then obtained by summing over all pairs:

\[
\mathrm{D\text{-}COMP}(G) = \sum_{i<j} C_{\mathrm{local}}(i, j)
\]

A high D-COMP value indicates a glyph with significant differential tension, shadow burden and recursion potential, requiring more energetic input from M.A.S. to stabilise. A low D-COMP value corresponds to a harmonious glyph that naturally fits within the Latin graphs and requires minimal correction.

This metric ties together the Q-vector assignments, D-state biases, frequency harmonics and the M.A.S. function, providing a single scalar measure of how demanding a glyph is on the lattice. In practice, the Dynamic Complexity guides how quickly recursion (Q$_3$) must be applied to avoid collapse and ensures the ALQC remains coherent.

\section{ALQC AND QUANTUM PHYSICS}\label{sec:quantum_physics}

Modern quantum mechanics is built on a small number of postulates. An isolated quantum system is represented by a vector in a complex Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$. The state vector $|\psi\rangle$ encapsulates all of the system's information up to a global phase.

\subsection{The Quantum Postulates in ALQC}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Composite Systems:} Represented on the tensor product of their component Hilbert spaces ($\mathcal{H}_A \otimes \mathcal{H}_B$). Entangled states cannot be factorized into separate subsystem vectors, and mixed states are described by positive trace-class density operators $\rho$.
    \item \textbf{Observables:} Physical observables are represented by Hermitian operators on the state space.
    \item \textbf{Measurement:} The outcomes of measurements are the operator's eigenvalues, and the Born rule assigns probabilities via the squared modulus of the projection of $|\psi\rangle$ onto the relevant eigenvectors.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Quantum Logic vs. ALQC}

r \land (p \lor q) \neq (r \land p) \lor (r \land q)

The orthomodular lattice of subspaces of Hilbert space replaces Boolean algebras as the structure of propositions. Within this landscape, the \textbf{Ahnend Logical Q-State Core} provides a quaternary logic that \textit{extends} quantum logic rather than competing with it.
